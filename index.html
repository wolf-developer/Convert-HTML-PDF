PCA and Ancestry

We assessed ancestry using principal components analysis (PCA) and iAdmix. Your genome was compared to a set of 26 global populations to provide an estimate of the components of their genetic ancestry. We use this ancestry inference to adjust an individual’s PRS score to account for their ancestry. Ancestry specific PRS distributions are built using populations with a range of ancestries and then used to adjust your score.

COMMON QUESTIONS

What is PRS?

A PRS is a number calculated from a person’s genetic data and is the sum of the number of risk alleles associated with the disease of interest weighted by the corresponding effect sizes (beta parameter) of those alleles on the disease.

How PRS percentile is calculated?

The PRS Percentile was computed using an ancestry adjusted Z-score based on the UK Biobank European samples distribution.

How Risk by Age 75 was calculated?

Risk by Age 75 was computed using a COX regression model using PRS as independent variable and adjusted for the first 4 principal components of variation and sex (when relevant)

What does it mean to have high lifetime genetic risk?

If you calculate PRS on a population the scores will form a normal distribution with most people having scores in the middle of this distribution. A high PRS means that your score is higher than many in the population. This equates to your genes giving you a comparatively higher than average risk compared to the population. We define High lifetime genetic risk as a lifetime risk that is at least 3 times higher than the risk for the remainder of the population. Having a high lifetime genetic risk does not mean that the disease will definitely develop, as there are numerous additional lifestyle and physiological factors that contribute to modify the absolute risk. Additionally, the risk for many diseases varies substantially with age.

TEST LIMITATIONS

It is important to note that the results of the PRS analyses presented here are not diagnostic. The aim of these genetic and bioinformatic analyses is to provide additional information to clinicians about the risk of disease to a patient that is conferred by their genes. Depending on the PRS, the analyses take into account a range of between tens to millions common genetic variants that have been robustly associated with the disease of interest. However, there may still be additional, as yet unidentified, genetic variants involved in genetic risk. Furthermore, our PRS do not currently take into account rare variants in known disease-associated genes, such as LDLR and other genes that cause familial hypercholesterolemia in the case of CAD, or BRCA1 in the case of BC. It is likely that pathogenic rare variants will also contribute to an individual's genetic risk of developing a disease.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE

Large-scale prospective studies (i.e., sample sizes higher than 450.000 individuals) have demonstrated that individuals with high PRS for some diseases display a significant higher risk of disease. This includes CAD (Khera et al 2018; Sun et al 2021)

TECHNICAL DETAILS

Our analyses rely on genetic data that can come from a variety of sources including genotyping arrays, or either high- or low-coverage whole genome sequence data. We use software called Beagle V.5 to impute this genetic data into a common set of 50 million genetic variants. The reference panel used for imputation comprises 2,504 fully sequenced and haplotypically phased genomes from Phase 3 of 1000 Genomes project. Our PRS have been calculated based on panels of varying numbers of Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs), details of which are provided in each PRS report. The sum of the number of risk alleles for each SNP is weighted by the corresponding effect size from the PRS panel. We select SNPs and associated effect sizes from the largest available Genome Wide Association Study (GWAS) available for a particular disease and use a suite of PRS methods to identify the best performing PRS.

PRS PREDICTIVE PERFORMANCE

To provide insight on the predictive performance of our PRS we report the Area Under the Curve (AUC) of the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC). This tells us how good the PRS is at classifying known cases and controls for a disease, which we compute using a logistic regression model on scores computed from a population sample of 434,837 European-ancestry individuals from the UK Biobank.
